When Anil Kumar Antony walked into BJP headquarters in New Delhi on the last day of January 2023, he carried with him more than his own political ambitions. He carried the weight of a surname that had come to define six decades of Congress loyalty in Kerala — and the charge that he had just detonated it.

His father, A.K. Antony, was not just a former Chief Minister and Union Defence Minister. He was, for many in the Congress rank-and-file, the last living embodiment of incorruptibility in the party. To be AK's son was to occupy a special position in Kerala political society — protected by legacy, scrutinised by the same. Anil had grown up understanding that inheritance.

## The Digital Outpost

Anil Antony's formal Congress journey began in 2018 when he was appointed Convener of the KPCC's Digital Media wing. The role suited his profile: English-educated, socially fluent, media-savvy in ways that older KPCC functionaries often were not. He understood Twitter — then still the primary arena of Indian English political discourse — and could match the BJP IT cell's pace in ways that the party's traditional communicators struggled to.

He built a small but capable digital unit for the KPCC. He trained volunteers in social media monitoring, counter-messaging, and rapid response. In internal party circles, he was regarded as a capable organiser with access, via his family name, to the Congress high command that most state-level operatives could only dream of. He attended AICC events, maintained a visible Delhi presence, and cultivated relationships with national party spokespeople.

The role was not a stepping stone to electoral politics — Anil was never a candidate — but it was a meaningful organisational position, and he discharged it with measurable commitment over four years.

## The BBC Documentary and the Thread That Ended It

In January 2023, the BBC released a two-part documentary titled *India: The Modi Question*, which examined the 2002 Gujarat riots and raised questions about then-Chief Minister Narendra Modi's handling of the violence. The documentary was banned by the Indian government; universities that attempted to screen it faced administration pressure and, in some cases, physical disruption.

Congress, predictably, defended the documentary's right to be screened and raised the matter in Parliament. The party's public position was unambiguous: the ban was an assault on press freedom.

Anil Antony disagreed — publicly, and in a way that made walking back impossible.

On January 21, 2023, he published a lengthy Twitter thread arguing that the BBC documentary was "colonial propaganda" and that the Congress party was wrong to give it legitimacy. He praised Prime Minister Modi's international image and argued that any party that genuinely cared about India's global standing should distance itself from the film.

The thread was widely circulated, picked up by BJP handles, and celebrated by the party's social media apparatus as evidence of honest disagreement from within the Congress ecosystem. For the KPCC leadership, it was a straightforward case of disciplinary action.

## Expulsion and the Move to Delhi

The KPCC issued an expulsion notice within days. The charge was "anti-party activities" — specifically the public statements at odds with the party's official position. Anil Antony did not contest the notice. He did not issue a clarification, nor did he seek mediation through his father's channels, though observers noted that AK Antony made no public statement on his son's behalf.

On January 31, 2023, Anil Antony joined the BJP at a formal Delhi ceremony attended by senior party functionaries. He was received not as a lower-rung catch but as a symbolically significant acquisition. The optics were deliberate: the son of AK Antony, the man who had once chaired the Congress's 2014 election post-mortem panel, now standing under saffron banners.

Within weeks, he was appointed BJP National Spokesperson — a role that placed him on national television panels as an English-language face of the party.

## The Spokesperson Years

From 2023 onwards, Anil Antony occupied a visible position in BJP's media machinery. He appeared regularly on English news channels as the party's articulate, calm-voiced defender on topics ranging from constitutional questions to the Congress party's internal dysfunction. His background gave him credibility with journalists who knew his lineage; his departure from that lineage gave him a particular rhetorical utility — the convert's testimony.

He was careful, in most of his public appearances, to avoid directly criticising his father. AK Antony remained a Congress loyalist and made occasional statements distancing himself from his son's political choices without personalising the break. The Antony family's political divorce, publicly managed with remarkable restraint by both sides, became a minor study in how political families navigate defection.

## Significance

Anil Antony's defection was not the most senior Congress figure to join BJP in Kerala, but it was among the most symbolically legible. It demonstrated that BJP's recruitment was not limited to opportunists from the margins of the Congress coalition — it could reach into the party's most carefully cultivated legacy families and extract someone willing to make the break visible and loud.

It also demonstrated the mechanism by which KPCC discipline — in this case, a Twitter thread — could function as the actual instrument of defection. The expulsion did not stop Anil Antony from going to BJP. It may have accelerated the timeline and removed any ambiguity about the break.

The Kerala Pradesh Congress Committee, which prides itself on disciplinary rigour, had once again enforced a rule that handed a recruit to its principal opponent.
