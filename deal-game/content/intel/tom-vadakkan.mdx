Tom Vadakkan spent nearly a decade as one of the most recognisable faces of the Indian National Congress on national television. He had sat across from BJP spokespersons in the green rooms of every major English-language news channel, defended the Congress party's positions on defence, foreign policy, and the economy, and was regarded within the party as a reliable, calm, and articulate national-level communicator.

On February 28, 2019, he was no longer any of those things. He was the newest BJP National Spokesperson.

## The Kerala-to-Delhi Trajectory

Tom Vadakkan's Congress journey traced the path taken by several Kerala-origin politicians who found their careers not in the state assembly but in the national media-political ecosystem that Delhi produces. From Thrissur, where he had community and organisational roots, he had risen through the party's national communications apparatus to become an AICC spokesperson — one of the national faces deployed on behalf of the party at a national level.

His background as a Christian community leader from central Kerala gave him a dual utility. He could speak to national audiences on defence and governance, and he was also part of the party's outreach to Kerala's substantial Christian electorate, which the Congress had historically relied upon as part of its UDF coalition. He was not merely a media performer; he was an organisational asset with community ties.

By 2018, he had been a Congress spokesperson for close to nine years. He had defended the party through the UPA-2 coalition controversies, the 2014 Lok Sabha rout, and the subsequent years of opposition politics. He knew the party's talking points and its vulnerabilities, and he had remained committed to its public positions through several difficult electoral cycles.

## The Pulwama Context

On February 14, 2019, a suicide bombing in Pulwama district of Jammu and Kashmir killed 40 CRPF personnel. The attack was claimed by the Pakistan-based militant group Jaish-e-Mohammed and became, in the weeks that followed, the dominant fact of Indian political life in the run-up to the Lok Sabha elections.

The BJP government's response — including airstrikes in Balakot, Pakistan, on February 26 — generated both popular support and international scrutiny. Congress's handling of the post-Pulwama period was careful to the point of being perceived by some as equivocal. The party asked questions about the government's intelligence failures, cast doubt on the death toll figures, and raised procedural concerns about the Balakot operation.

Within the party's own ranks, this approach generated friction. Several Congress leaders privately felt that the cautious, questioning posture was politically damaging — that being seen to doubt the national security narrative in the immediate aftermath of a major terrorist attack carried serious electoral costs.

Tom Vadakkan was not cautious in expressing this view. He made his position known, according to sources cited at the time, to party leadership. He argued that the Congress party's public response to Pulwama was damaging its national security credentials and that the party should more clearly support the Indian armed forces' operations, whatever questions it might separately raise about governance.

The party did not change its position. Vadakkan did.

## The Departure

On February 28, 2019, twelve days after Pulwama and two days after the Balakot airstrikes, Tom Vadakkan submitted his resignation from the Congress party and joined the BJP. The timing was precise and, critics noted, politically advantageous: the national mood in the days following the airstrikes was one of unambiguous support for the military operation, and joining BJP in that moment carried maximum symbolic weight.

At his BJP induction event, he cited Congress's Pulwama response as his primary reason for leaving. He stated that he could not continue representing a party whose stance on national security he fundamentally disagreed with, and praised Prime Minister Modi's handling of the crisis as decisive and appropriate.

Congress leaders dismissed his stated reasons as post-hoc rationalisation of a pre-planned move. Several noted that he had shown no prior indication of national security disagreements during nearly a decade of party work. The coincidence of his departure with the peak of BJP's Pulwama-linked electoral momentum was pointed out by multiple AICC officials.

## Reception and Continued Role

BJP received Vadakkan's defection as a significant acquisition. A national-level Congress spokesperson joining the saffron camp weeks before the Lok Sabha elections was an optics win — it allowed BJP to argue that even Congress's own communicators were abandoning ship on the party's national security positions.

He was given the role of BJP National Spokesperson almost immediately, placing him on the same circuit of television panels he had previously occupied as a Congress representative. The transition was seamless in structural terms: the studios were the same, the interviewers were the same, the debating format was the same. The party colours had changed.

As of 2026, Tom Vadakkan remains an active BJP National Spokesperson. He has been deployed regularly on television and in party communications, particularly on topics related to national security, the UDF coalition in Kerala, and the Congress party's organisational decline.

## The Pattern He Exemplified

Tom Vadakkan's defection was significant beyond his individual career. He represented a type: the national-level Congress communicator from Kerala who finds, after sustained engagement with the party's direction, that he has more in common with the BJP's positions on certain issues than with the party he has been defending.

The mechanism — a triggering event, a public statement of disagreement, a clean departure — was efficient. It generated maximum media coverage with minimum internal procedural drama. By contrast with the more drawn-out defections that involved expulsions or factional battles, Vadakkan simply left. And BJP, ready with a welcome and a role, converted the departure immediately.

It was a template that would be deployed again, in different forms, in the years that followed.
